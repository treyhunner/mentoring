Be a Learning Ally

- When you're coaching someone, **I don't want you to be an expert**, I want you to be an ally in learning - Which means...

You're not the star of the show

- You're not the star of the show: you're a side character - Your job is **to support and guide your learner**, but they shouldn't be dependent upon you - This process is centered around your learners, not you - ...

Teach, don't tell

"You learn by doing"

- I often live code when I teach. - I'm always tempted to write code out and then explain it, but that's not teaching **(click)**, that's telling. **(click)** - I want you to *teach, not tell* **(click)** - You can do that by encouraging your learners to practice **information retrieval** - We don't learn by trying to *put knowledge into our heads* - We *learn* by attempting to retrieve knowledge *from* our heads - Who has heard the expression "learn by doing" **(click)**... you really *do* learn by *doing*: you learn by trying to take information out of your head and apply it - Which is tricky because new you're new to something, you're not good at **the doing** yet - And *doing* when **you don't know what you're doing** is intimidating - As a mentor, you can make this problem of *not knowing* a little less relevant by **encouraging your learner to make mistakes**

Encourage making mistakes

  • "I don't know, give it a try."
  • "What have you tried so far?"
- Making mistakes involves a *process* of considering what *might be* a correct answer and *what isn't*... and then *trying it out* - You're going to be wrong **most of the time**, but doesn't actually matter. The process matters. This process of trying, getting feedback, and trying again, **that's** a *learning process* - I don't just want you to just carve out a space where making mistakes is acceptable, I actually want you to actively *encourage* **(click)** making mistakes... just not using your production database - When doing this you could say things like ... - **(click)** "I don't know, give it a try" - Or **(click)** "what have you tried so far?" - You want to reinforce the fact that *playing* is the way learning happens - We learn by doing, and doing involves making *a lot* of mistakes - ... - So after your learners have made mistakes and they've found themselves stuck, I want you to...

Build Trust

- That your learner feels that they can trust you - Your learner is in a vulnerable situation - They need to feel they can trust you - One big way you can build trust, is ...

Be Vulnerable

  • Telling bad jokes
  • Making mistakes
  • Saying "I don't know"
- By being vulnerable yourself - There are a lot of ways to be vulnerable - For example, I make bad jokes **(click)**. They're not usually not very funny. That makes me a little bit vulnerable. - I also make mistakes, sometimes on purpose and most of the time on accident. But I don't ever "ah I did that on purpose to test you". I want my learners to *feel* my fallibility. - Another big one is saying "I don't know" - I don't know, but **let's try it out** - Or... I'm not sure, **let's look it up** - So vulnerability is a big way to build trust - Another big factor in being trusted is the way you speak

Speak Deliberately

  • "What does this do?"
  • "What will happen when we run this?"
  • "What do you think will happen when we run this?"
  • "What do you think might happen when we run this? What's your guess?"
- Here's 4 ways to say almost the same thing... - **4 Clicks** - There are times when it makes sense to have confident and assertive language that's to-the-point - That last way of saying this is welcoming, you're inviting your learner to guess - That first one is a test: that's a nerve-wracking way to ask that question - You want to quiz people, meaning you want them to practice information retrieval, but you don't want them to **feel like they're being tested** - Confident and assertive language has its place, but you want to *invite* your learners to make mistakes and **to guess** - So I want you to think about how what you're saying might be interpreted. Not what you mean, but what might be heard - Regardless of how you do this, I definitely want you to try to be **encouraging**

Encourage, don't alienate

  • "So obviously, ..."
  • "Of course, ..."
  • "Oh it's easy, you just... "
- There are a lot of ways you can alienate you learners, in big ways and in small ways - "So obviously" **(click)**, "of course" **(click)**, "it's easy, you just" **(click)** - Those are all examples of phrases you might want to avoid - When you're learning, very few things are **obvious** and most things **are not easy** - That's the whole reason you're learning - What's in your head as a mentor, is not in your learners head - What's easy for you, is not easy for your learner. It's hard. - Your learner doesn't know things that you do *yet* because they're still in the process of learning - *(breathe)* This next slide is the scariest one I have

Believe in your learners

- If you don't believe in your learner's ability to improve, they are less likely to improve their skills - Who has heard of the growth mindset? - If you adopt a growth mindset you believe that through directed effort, you can and you will improve your skills: that your skills aren't fixed but are expandable - There's research that backs up the idea that you're more likely to learn if you adopt a growth mindset: you're more likely to grow your skills if you believe in your ability to do so - Your belief in your learner is *also very important*: it's hard for your learner to believe in themselves, if you don't believe in them - They come from a baseline of understanding that's below yours: you can't judge that baseline, you just **have to believe** that they'll build up from where they're at - You need to convince yourself of your learner's abilities and give them the impression, explicitly or implicitly, that you believe in them

Slow Down

- Is slow down - I want you to slow down your thinking, slow down your speaking, slow down your acting - Especially when you're in a situation where you're not quite sure *how best to act*, slow down.

Reflect

- When you speak, **question what you're saying and why** - Act with intention: try to empathize with what your learner might be feeling - It can sometimes be a little distracting to do this too much in the moment, but you should at least take a little bit of time at some point to **reflect on what you're doing** so that you can improve - Reflection is an important part of learning, and *you* are learning too

Take things one step at a time

  1. A non-existent (misspelled) function being called
  2. Some code that's poorly styled, but syntactically valid
  3. An error in understanding of the technical requirements
  4. A syntax error that's stopping the code from running at all
- Let's say your learner says: "my code doesn't work" - They show you the code, and you see 4 things: - A function that's improperly named - Code that's a little ugly, but it works - Something that seems like a logical error: a misunderstanding in the actual problem you're solving - And a syntax error: something that stops the code from running at all - Which of these do you focus on? And which one do you bring up first? - I'd argue that last one is the most important *right now*: if you have a syntax error, Python won't let you get any further. - And that second one is maybe the least important. Code style is very important, but whether your code *works* is much more important. Until that point, talking about code style is just a distraction. You may not even want to bring up code style *at all* until you have time to make it the primary focus of your conversation.

Learn About Learning

- One thing to do while you're slowing down is to focus on the act of learning itself - There are a lot of people who've devoted their careers to improving the learning habits and teaching habits of others. - Try to borrow ideas from those people so that you can become better at teaching, but also so that you can help your learner become better at learning. - Learn about learning best practices so that you can share them - Talk about the growth mindset - Talk about the fact that you learn by retrieving information from your head, not by putting it in - I have some resources I'll recommend at the end that will help you learn more about learning theory - Remember that you're not just there to help your learner improve their programming skills, you're there to help them **become better at learning itself**. - Software development usually entails a life long devotion to learning. It's never too late to improve your learning skills.

Fake it until you make it

Adopt a growth mindset

- "Fake it until you make it" - I don't really like this phrase: I don't really like the connotation... but there is truth behind this idea - If you *slow yourself down* and think "if I were a *really good* mentor", you're sort of pretending you're a good mentor... you're faking it - But in the process you're trying to act deliberately, you're acting with intention - But you're also practicing, which is *part* of learning - As you continue to deliberately act a certain way, you'll eventually start to intuitively that act that way - Remember the growth mindset **(click)** - Mental ability can be learned. **Thoughfulness**, **empathy**... are learned skills. **Friendliness**... is a learned skill. - Congratulating your learners and feeling excited on their behalf... that's learned too.
